Anne Sneyd		 On December 2, 2015, we met as a team with Anne Sneyd, Director of Brokerage and Shipping at Canada Fibers Ltd. Anne has been working with Canada Fibres Ltd. for 9 years, and passed some of the knowledge she had gained about recycling in those 9 years on to us. She enlightened us on what Canada Fibres Ltd. is, how their facility operates, and gave us some useful information about the recycling stream, and how recycled materials are processed. We are thankful to Anne for sharing her knowledge with us, and providing us with feedback on our prototype. She informed us about the educational aspect of our trash bin and commented on how our bin helps the user learn where various waste items belong.


Alan Blake
 Alan Blake is the Executive Director of PAC NEXT. He contributes his 30 years of experience in the consumer goods industry including 20 years of global packaging design and development expertise to PAC NEXT. He collaborates with Rachel Morrier, another expert that we reached out to. He recommended that we have the Smart Sort Trash Bin, which currently sorts garbage by sorting it into either garbage, recycling, fibers, compost and glass, to having it also sort the items by their brand name. This addition to the Smart Sort Trash Bin makes it easier for the user to describe the product that they are disposing of.


James Downham		 Since 2006, Jim has been the President and CEO of Packaging Consortium (PAC), a not-for-profit organization. Jim's career was set in motion at Continental Can Company (CCC) in a management development program. He followed the sales and marketing career path and progressed through the ranks of Packaging Systems. This led to Vice Presidential roles of business units and ultimately to divisional President and CEO. On Wednesday November 11th Ctrl-Z met with Mr. Downham at our school Bayview Glen. He explained that PAC focuses on packaging waste, working with companies and educating consumers to create a world without packaging waste. We learned about how waste is collected and sorted and that contamination of recyclable materials recovered in a MRF (Municipal Recovery Facility) causes most valuable materials to be diverted to landfills. Recovery rates are very low because most people do not care about recycling and it is difficult to sort different types of materials. He also pointed out that 33 % of all food is thrown out or wasted.
